Genuary

MFA Thesis

The Quantified Self is best described by Gary Wolf as “self-knowledge through numbers.” William James’ theory of the consciousness of the self and the study of coping inspires drawings that retell personal narratives at the same time reveal traces of the subtle emotion associated with intentional forgetting. Codex Endogenous is a project that reveals and visualizes the beauty and morphology of a “self” and its environment. Here, “codex” refers to a collection of pages stitched together, and “endogenous” is a term in cognitive neuroscience used to describe phenomena that is spontaneously generated from an individual’s internal state. Every day, quantifiable information about the self is produced; left unrevealed would leave the spirit of a self undiscovered. In this project, the self information is transformed into self-knowledge. Data is tracked throughout a day, categorized into implicit and explicit information, visualized as daily data drawings. Analogous to the practice of keeping a daily journal, Codex Endogenous is a digital journal in a screen-based format. It is composed of drawings that embody data representation of the quantified self in the context of environmental habituation including the intentional redaction of information rather forgotten.

Theory of Mind Prism

Inspiration

Networks, emotion and behavior became a determining factor of interest for UC Davis' Annual Community Book Project centered around the idea of Joy. Theory of mind is the human phenomena that allows us to detect one another’s emotions. People were able to move around freely and watch trails of lines connect to nearby beings. Slowly the colors would layer on top of eachother and morph into new colors. Euclidian relationships acted as an abstract analogy to the social relationships we form

First acccomplished hard coded blob detection with persistance process shot. Followed Daniel Shiffman's tutorial and integrsated it with my own design. Counts current blobs in the space and deletes blobs when they disappear.

Implementation

Processing drew on to a canvas using spatial input from a kinect ir sensor. Within processing, the ir sensor took a bitmapped grid of pixels, mapped a retangle around the max distances on the x and y axis and calculated the center to draw a point, thus forming lines segments from one vector to the next. The bit map mirroring a person showed up as grey and the lines were colored by a spectrum class that preformed a function to iterate over the hsb spectrum. This resulted in trails of prismatic connections. For more implementation visit

Pitter Patter

Inspiration

I was invited to participate in a series at The Exploratorium about emergent new media technology for storytelling. My first dry summer after moving from the east coast, I was left nostalgic for rain. I created an environment that used the current number of people in the room to power a rainstorm. The more people in the room, the more calm the storm becomes encouraging people to gather around the structure almost like a beacon. A the bottom of the fabric are screen prints of native Californian plants printed in Kala Art Institute in Berkeley.

Implementation

Pitter patter is an iteration of the same projection methods used in Aurora Print but instead using larger panels of fabric. The input is coming from a kinect device that is tracking people in 3D space. A TSPS software was used as a library in a webpage that recorded number of people in the room and age or time in the space. Javascript is the main engine that powered the simulation of rain using processing. For more implementation visit

AuroraPrint

Inspiration

Aurora Print was my first time tinkering with physical computing for a large scale installation. The idea started after watching Don Hertzfeldt’s A World of Tomorrow. The line “I opened up a gallery of anonymous memories” had become stuck in my mind and inspired me to create my own version. Vague but collosal memories accumulate when you start to acclimate to a new zone, or geographical area. I wanted to change perspective to the idea of sharing one's own memories. The structure hung from the ceiling all on the same x plane. But at different x and y planes forming an environment inn which you stare upward, thus an aurora state of memories.

Implementation

The project utilizes multiple facets of computational methods. A pulse sensor takes heartbeat input as a method of animating a still image. RGB channels, or the three channels of color often to decsribe color on the web are warped and shifted using GLSL Shaders. A projector uses madmapper to use syphon input to display a web page onto fabric hangings.

Alexa is a creative coder and design researcher who has spent her career programming mixed reality large-scale and screen-based data visualizations representing the "quantified self". Beginning in the Baltimore art scene, Alexa started a personal practice of meditational drawings and self-teaching herself code before moving to the Bay Area. She has exhibited work in and around San Francisco and East Bay including Kala Art Institute, Gray Area Foundation for the Arts, and the Exploratorium. Alexa has completed her research at UC Davis with a focus on data visualization and personal science. When she is not teaching a digital design course, she spends her time prototyping, experimental art practices, and building code. Alexa's current research consists of remodeling ways to shift conventional uses of common technologies that are meant for the high-functioning world to provide self-knowledge and self-care to individuals who are neurodivergent or have neurodegenerative diseases.
